\section{Introduction}

In phylogenetics, we have a collection of objects whose ancestral history we hope to infer
using only modern-day information. This shared history is summarized by a \textit{phylogenetic tree},
a graphical tree whose leaves are labeled by these objects. Classically, these objects represented different biological species,
the relevant data was their DNA, and the phylogenetic tree encoded the sequence of
speciation events that occurred over time to produce the species we see today. Nonetheless, the tools of phylogenetics are far 
more broadly applicable: they have been used in medicine to better understand the 
progression of cancer~\cite{somarelli2017phylooncology}, in astrophysics as a hierarchical clustering procedure for astral
 bodies~\cite{fraixburnet2015multivariate}, and in terrane analysis to study the geological history of specific 
 regions~\cite{michaux2018cladistic}. 

In this chapter, we study a specific application to linguistics, where the objects of interest are different 
languages and the underlying data are \textit{linguistic characters}. These characters encode features 
that vary across languages and are commonly divided into three broad categories: \textit{phonological},
 \textit{morphological}, and \textit{lexical} characters. 
 
 Phonological characters describe sound patterns, 
 such as whether a language distinguishes tones or permits consonant clusters. Morphological characters 
 describe grammatical structure, such as how past tense or plural number is expressed. Lexical characters describe the word 
 forms used to express specific ideas, often by grouping languages according to whether their words for 
  common concepts such as ``fire,'' or ``mother'' descend from a common historical source. Lexical characters are 
  particularly interesting as they can be multi-state---several different words can simultaneously share 
  the same meaning---a property known as \textit{polymorphism}.  We are specifically interested in a model known as the \textit{Canby et al. model}, introduced in 
\cite{polymorphism_linguistic_phylogenetics}, which explicitly models this polymorphism.

% Canby Model

\subsection{The Canby et al. Model}

The key feature of the Canby model is that a language may carry a set of states for a given
character, and these states may be born or die as the character evolves along the tree.
Suppose that $\cT^*$ is the underlying rooted phylogenetic tree of languages. Given some collection of characters $\cC$, the Canby model~\cite{polymorphism_linguistic_phylogenetics}
assumes each character $c \in \cC$ evolves down this tree according to a simple birth-death process independently in each branch. Namely, associated to each character $c$ is a set $\Sigma^{(c)}$
of possible states, birth/death rates $\{\lambda_i^{(c)}\}, \{\mu_i^{(c)}\}$, a probability measure $\nu^{(c)}$ 
on $\Sigma^{(c)}$ for selecting new states, and a probability measure $\rho^{(c)}$ on the set ${\Sigma^{(c)} \choose *}$ of nonempty finite subsets of $\Sigma^{(c)}$ for 
determining the multi-state of the root. Let $[0, \ell]$ denote the unique path from the root of $\cT^*$ to some leaf $\ell \in \cT$. For $x \in [0, \ell]$ we assume the
multi-state $c(x)$ for the given character evolves down this path as follows
%
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
    \item The multi-state $c(0)$ of the root is sampled from $\rho^{(c)}$. 
    \item If $|c(x)| = i$, at rate $\lambda_i^{(c)}$ a new character is born, and at rate $\mu_i^{(c)}$ a character will die.
    \item If a death occurs, a state $\alpha \in c(x)$ is chosen uniformly at random to delete. If a birth occurs, a new state $\alpha \notin c(x)$ is chosen proportional to $\nu^{(c)}$, namely via
                \[\mu(A) := \frac{\nu^{(c)}(A \setminus c(x))}{\nu^{(c)}(\Sigma^{(c)} \setminus c(x))}\]
\end{enumerate}
%
When a branch-point $v$ of $\cT^*$ is reached, the multi-state evolves independently down each child branch starting from
state $c(v)$. 

A state $\alpha \in \Sigma^{(c)}$ with $\nu^{(c)}(\{\alpha\}) > 0$ is called \textit{homoplastic}. These states
can evolve independently in different lineages, rather than from shared ancestry. We denote the set of homoplastic states
and non-homoplastic states by $\Sigma_H^{(c)}, \Sigma_N^{(c)}$ respectively. Homoplastic states in a sense complicate the 
inference process, as two languages can share the homoplastic trait without necessarily having any recent shared ancestry.
 
Throughout this paper, we will assume characters follow the \textit{immigration-death rates}
%
\begin{equation}
    \lambda_i^{(c)} = \lambda^{(c)}, \quad \mu_i^{(c)} = i \mu^{(c)}.
\end{equation}
%
\noindent for $i > 1$. Namely, the birth-rate is constant, and the death rate is proportional to the number of individuals. 
Additionally, we will assume that $\mu_1^{(c)} = 0$. This ensures that each character
has at least one state at any point in time, and that the number of states cannot blow up in finite time.  

Since the upcoming inference procedure is based solely on non-homoplastic states, we also make the
following assumptions about $\nu^{(c)}$ and $\rho^{(c)}$.

\begin{assumption}[1]
    Non-homoplastic states occur sufficiently often. Namely
    %
    \begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
        \item There is positive probability that the macro-state at the root contains at
              least one micro-state that is not homoplastic
              %
                \[\rho^{(c)} \left(\left\{A \in {\Sigma^{(c)} \choose *} \; : \; A \cap \Sigma^{(c)}_N \neq \emptyset \right\}\right) > 0.\]
              %
        \item There is positive probability of adding a micro-state that is not homo-
                plastic to the current macro-state when there is a birth
                %
                \[\nu^{(c)}(\Sigma_N^{(c)}) > 0.\]
                %
    \end{enumerate}
\end{assumption}

The full Canby model additionally incorporates another mechanism of character transmission known
as \textit{borrowing} which allows characters to transfer laterally throughout the tree, analogous to
horizontal gene transfer in biological contexts. In this paper, we only consider the case where no borrowing
occurs, but interested readers can find further details in \cite{polymorphism_linguistic_phylogenetics}.

For ease of notation, when the meaning is clear from context we typically conflate the multi-state $c(x)$ of 
a given character at location $x \in \cT^*$ with $x$ itself.

\subsection{ASTRAL}

To infer the underlying tree from many independently evolving characters, we adapt ideas from
quartet-based species-tree estimation. In phylogenetic inference, one often wants to combine information from many sources, such as genetic information across
a collection of genes or, in our case, linguistic characters. In \textit{consensus methods}, each source is
used to generate an estimate $g_i$ of the true phylogenetic tree $\cT^*$, and then these estimates $\{g_i\}_{i=1}^n$
are aggregated. In the phylogenetics literature, the trees $g_i$ are called the \textit{gene trees}, while $\cT^*$ is
referred to as the \textit{species tree}. 

ASTRAL~\cite{mirarab2014astral} is one commonly used aggregation procedure. It scores candidate 
phylogenetic trees $\cT$ based on the number of quartets they share with the estimated trees $\{g_i\}$. Specifically,
it solves the optimization problem
%
\begin{equation}
    \hat{T} = \argmax_\cT \sum_{i=1}^n \sum_{q \in \cQ(\cT)} 1 \{q \in g_i\}.
    \label{eqn:astral_quartet_support_optimization_problem}
\end{equation}
%
\noindent Since the set of phylogenetic trees is super-exponentially large, it is intractable to solve this 
optimization problem exactly for trees with more than 20 or so leaves. For larger trees, a
restricted search space is required. 

One possible strategy is to limit the bipartitions of the leaves the candidate trees $\cT$ can induce. 
A \textit{bipartition} of a set $S$ is a partition of $S = A \cup B$ into two nonempty, disjoint subsets. 
Every edge $e \in E(\cT)$ of our tree induces a bipartition of the leaves by considering the two connected components
of the graph $(V(\cT), E(\cT) \setminus \{e\})$. Moreover, Buneman's splits-equivalence theorem shows $\cT$
is completely characterized by its set $\cX(\cT)$ of bipartitions. 

Since each $g_i$ is a noisy estimate of $\cT^*$, we 
might expect that $\cX(\cT^*) \subseteq \cX_g := \bigcup_i \cX(g_i)$, suggesting
the restricted search space
%
\[\Big\{\cT \; : \; \cX(\cT) \subseteq \bigcup_{i=1}^n \cX(g_i)\Big\}.\]
%
\noindent More generally, given any set $\cX$ of bipartitions, we can solve
%
%
\begin{equation}
    \hat{T} = \argmax_{\cX(\cT) \subseteq \cX} \sum_{i=1}^n \sum_{q \in \cQ(\cT)} 1 \{q \in g_i\}.
    \label{eqn:astral_heuristic_optimization_problem}
\end{equation}
%
%
Using a clever dynamic-programming principle, ASTRAL efficiently solves this bipartition-constrained 
optimization problem. For polynomially-sized $\cX$, the optimization problem~\eqref{eqn:astral_heuristic_optimization_problem}
can be feasibly solved for trees with thousands of leaves. Moreover, when the trees $g_i$ are generated under the 
multispecies coalescent model \cite{rannala2020msc}, \cite{hill2020speciestreeestimationjoint}, its statistical properties 
are well-understood. In particular, it is asymptotically consistent whenever $\cX_g \subseteq \cX$. 

The paper~\cite{shekhar2018_how_many_genes_are_enough} showed for the exact version~\eqref{eqn:astral_quartet_support_optimization_problem}
that $n = \Theta(f^{-2} \log(k))$ independent gene trees $g_i$ are required to recover $\cT^*$ with high probability, where $f$ is the minimum
branch length in the tree. Nonetheless, it has been empirically observed that the relaxed version~\eqref{eqn:astral_heuristic_optimization_problem} requires
significantly more data to obtain similar recovery rates~\cite{shekhar2018_how_many_genes_are_enough}. 

As discussed in \cite{uricchio2016_bipartition_cover}, \cite{mcnulty2026improvedbipartitioncoverbound}, the main shortcoming of the relaxed problem
is that it is quite unlikely that $\cX(T) \subseteq \cX_g$, since the bipartitions induced by edges near the root are very 
difficult to recover. Several heuristics have been proposed to extend the set $\cX_g$ in an effort to improve its coverage 
properties and the empirical performance of the relaxed problem~\cite{mirarab2015astral}, \cite{zhang2018astral}, \cite{dibaeinia2021fastral}. 
Alternatively \cite{Zhang2022_weighted_ASTRAL} proposes a weighted version of~\eqref{eqn:astral_quartet_support_optimization_problem}
which incorporates quartet-support information from the gene-tree estimation stage.

\subsection{ASTRAL-Based Inference in the Canby Model}

For data generated under the Canby model, quartet support can be defined directly from the
shared non-homoplastic states of the observed languages. Let $q = uv||xy$ denote the unrooted
quartet topology that groups $u$ with $v$ and $x$ with $y$.

\begin{center}
\begin{tikzpicture}[scale=0.9, every node/.style={font=\small}]
    \coordinate (a) at (-1, 0);
    \coordinate (b) at (1, 0);
    \coordinate (u) at (-2.5, 0.9);
    \coordinate (v) at (-2.5, -0.9);
    \coordinate (x) at (2.5, 0.9);
    \coordinate (y) at (2.5, -0.9);

    \draw (u) -- (a) -- (b) -- (x);
    \draw (v) -- (a);
    \draw (y) -- (b);

    \node[left] at (u) {$u$};
    \node[left] at (v) {$v$};
    \node[right] at (x) {$x$};
    \node[right] at (y) {$y$};
\end{tikzpicture}
\end{center}

\noindent Given a character $c$, define the support $M^{(c)}_q$ of the quartet $q$ via
%
\begin{equation*}
    M^{(c)}_{uv||xy}:= \Big\{(\alpha, \beta) \in \Sigma_N \times \Sigma_N \; : \; \alpha \neq \beta, \quad \alpha \in u \cap v \cap \bar{x} \cap \bar{y}, \quad \beta \in \bar{u} \cap \bar{v} \cap x \cap y\Big\}. 
\end{equation*}
%
Namely, $M_q$ consists of the pairs of non-homoplastic states that are seemingly ``split'' across the quartet $q$.
For the true quartet topology, this support is larger in expectation than the support assigned to
the two alternatives:
%
\begin{lemma}
    If $uv||xy$ is the quartet topology of the leaves $(u,v,x,y)$ in $\cT^*$ then
    \[\E M_{uv || xy} > \E M_{ux || vy} = \E M_{uy || vx},\]
    %
    \noindent so the correct topology has the highest expected support.
    \label{lem:correct_topo_max_expected_support}
\end{lemma}
%
\noindent For each $S = \{u,v,x,y\} \in {[k] \choose 4}$ 
they then let
%
\begin{equation}
    q_S := \argmax_{q \in A_S} \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n M_q^{(c_i)}, \quad A_S := \{uv||xy, ux||vy, uy||vx\}.
    \label{eqn:astral_based_canby_inference_original}
\end{equation}
%
\noindent By the law of large numbers, the correct topology becomes overwhelmingly likely for large $n$. 
They then run ASTRAL on the quartet set $\{q_S\}$
to generate an estimate $\hat{\cT}$ of $T^*$.

\subsection{Contributions}

We now outline the remainder of the chapter and summarize its main contributions.

\begin{itemize}

    \item We develop a set $\cX$ of bipartitions that can be used to restrict ASTRAL, 
    and show that it can (i) be computed in polynomial time, (ii) preserve asymptotic consistency,
    and (iii) remain small enough to be computationally useful. We propose a simple candidate, as well as
    several heuristics for improving its empirical coverage. 

    \item We develop sample-complexity bounds analogous to \cite{shekhar2018_how_many_genes_are_enough}
          to quantify how many characters are required to recover the underlying phylogenetic tree
          with high probability. 

    \item We introduce an alternative ASTRAL-based inference procedure for Canby-model data.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Coupling to Immigration-Death Processes}

The constraint $\mu_1 = 0$ makes the Canby model hard to analyze, because it adds a certain
dependence structure between the different states of a specific character. If we drop this condition, we 
get a classical immigration death (ID) process, which is quite tractable to analyze. This approximation
is reasonable in the parameter regime where the number of characters in a given branch is rarely one. 

Our key tool will be a coupling of the Canby model to a standard immigration-death process (ID) run along the branches of our species tree $T^*$. It relies on two simple observations about how the set of nonhomoplastic states evolves over time:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Births always occur at rate at least $\lambda_N := \lambda \nu(\Sigma_N)$ and at most $\lambda$.

    \item Deaths always occur at rate at most $\mu$ per individual.
\end{itemize}
%
\noindent In particular, we can couple an ID process $(Y_s)_{s \in T^*}$ to our Canby $(X_s)_{s \in T^*}$ so that:
%
\[ Y_s \cap \Sigma_N \subseteq X_s \cap \Sigma_N, \quad \forall s \in T^*\]
%
\noindent In this way, we only reduce the number of nonhomoplastic states we observe in the leaves, which heuristically makes inference more challenging. 

To produce this coupling, use a single $\tau \sim \mathrm{\Exp}(\lambda)$ clock for each branch of $T^*$ to determine possible births. If $\tau$ rings at location $s \in T^*$, sample a new state $\alpha \notin X_{s^-}$ for the Canby model as usual, namely according to the conditional distribution:
%
\[\frac{\nu(\cdot \cap (\Sigma \setminus X_{s^-}))}{\nu( \Sigma \setminus X_{s^-})}\]
%
\noindent Hence we let $X_s = X_{s^-} \cup \{\alpha\}$. We update our ID process $Y_s$ as follows: if $\alpha \notin \Sigma_N$, then let $Y_s = Y_{s^-}$. Namely, ignore the homoplastic states. Otherwise:
%
\[Y_s = \begin{cases}
    Y_{s^-} \cup \{\alpha\} & \mathrm{w.p.}  \quad \nu(\Sigma \setminus X_{s^-})\\
    Y_{s^-} & \mathrm{w.p.} \quad 1-\nu(\Sigma \setminus X_{s^-}) \\
\end{cases}\]
%
\noindent This ensures that the birth rate in $(Y_s)$ along a given branch is always $\lambda_N$. 

Similarly, when a new state $\alpha$ is born in $X_s$, assign it an independent $\tau_\alpha \sim \mathrm{Exp(\mu)}$ clock. If it rings at location $s \in T^*$, let:
%
\[X_s = \begin{cases}
    X_{s^-} \setminus \{\alpha\} & X_{s-} \neq \{\alpha\}\\
    \{\alpha\} & \mathrm{otherwise}
\end{cases}\]
%
\noindent Then let $Y_s = Y_{s^-} \setminus \{\alpha\}$. Namely, $(X_s)$ simply ignores the deaths causing the state set to become empty, while $(Y_s)$ does not.

coupling we introduced in the previous section.

of states in the multi-state. This is a simple birth-death process with birth rates 
$\lambda_i \equiv \lambda$ and death rates $\mu_i = i\mu \cdot 1\{i > 1\}$. Hence

% stationary distribution
\begin{lemma}
    The stationary distribution of $S_t$ is $ \pi = (Z | Z > 0)$ for $Z \sim \mathrm{Poisson}(\lambda/\mu)$. 
    %
    \[\pi_i = \frac{(\lambda/\mu)^i}{i!(e^{\lambda/\mu}-1)}, \quad \forall i \geq 1.\]
    %
    \label{lem:stationary_dist_state_cardinality_process}
\end{lemma}

\noindent The ergodic theorem tells us as $T \to \infty$ the fraction of the time $\{S_t\}_{t \in [0,T]}$ spends in state 1

%
\[\mu_{\text{eff}} = \mu \left(1-\pi_1\right) = \mu \cdot \frac{e^m-m-1}{e^m-1}\]
%
\noindent If $\lambda \ll \mu$, then $\mu_{\text{eff}} \approx \frac{\lambda}{2} $ which is significantly less than $\mu$ in this low-polymorphism regime. \section{A Restricted Search-Space}

\noindent As we discussed in the introduction, the exact version of ASTRAL~\eqref{eqn:astral_quartet_support_optimization_problem}
becomes intractable to solve even for trees with a modest number of leaves. Instead, we must construct a 
set $\cX$ of bipartitions which will limit the search space for the true tree $\cT$, which is the content of this section. 

To this effect, one useful class of sets when it comes to building $\cX$ are the leaf sets:
%
\[L_\alpha := \{\text{leaves }\ell : \alpha \in c(\ell)\}\]
%
\noindent Say a subset $L \subseteq S$ of leaves is \textit{contained} within a given bipartition $\phi := (A|B)$ if:
%
\[L_\alpha \subseteq A \qquad \text{or} \qquad L_\alpha \subseteq B\]
%
It is easy to see for nonhomoplastic states $\alpha \in \Sigma_N $, if $\alpha$ is born
 on the edge $e$ and $\phi_e = (A_e | S-A_e)$ is the bipartition this edge 
 generates in $\cT^*$ then $L_\alpha$ is contained in $\phi_e$. Moreover, if no
deaths of $\alpha$ occur after its born then in fact $L_\alpha \in \{A_e, S-A_e\}$. Hence 
one choice for the candidate bipartitions is
%
\[\cX_1 := \{(L^{(c)}_\alpha|S-L_\alpha) : c \in C, \alpha \in \Sigma^{(c)}\}\]
%
\noindent Trivially $|\cX_1| = O(|C|)$, so its often much smaller than the 
total $2^k$ number of possible bipartitions. It is easy to see under mild 
assumptions this alone is sufficient for ensuring statistical consistency, 
since with positive probability a given non-homoplastic $\alpha$ is born 
along an edge $e$ and does not die at all. More granullary

% bipartition cover bound
\begin{theorem}[Bipartition Cover Bounds for Canby With No Borrowing]
Let $\cB$ be the set of bipartitions of a phylogenetic tree $\cT$ with $k$ leaves and minimum/maximum branch lengths $T_{min}, T_{max}$ respectively. Suppose further we have a Canby model $\left\{\Sigma^{(c)}, \lambda^{(c)}, \mu^{(c)}, \rho^{(c)}\right\}_{c \in C}$ on $\cT$ with no borrowing and whose rates follow an immigration-death process. Let:
%
\[C_N := \{c \in C \; : \; \nu^{(c)}(\Sigma_N^{(c)}) > 0\}\]
%
\noindent be the set of characters with positive chance of non-homoplasy. Furthermore, assume we have the following assumptions on the parameters of the Canby model:

\begin{enumerate}
    
    \item[(i)] \textit{Nonvanishing Birth Rates:} Suppose that $\lambda^{(c)} \nu^{(c)}(\Sigma_N^{(c)}) \geq b > 0$ for all $c \in C_N$.

    \item[(ii)] \textit{Bounded death rates}: Suppose $\mu^{(c)} \leq D < \infty$ for all $c \in C_N$. 
\end{enumerate}
%
\noindent Then we have exponential convergence of the coverage probability:
%
\[\P(\cB \nsubseteq \cX_1)  \leq\sum_{\ell=2}^{k-2} q_\ell^{|C_N|} \leq (k-3)q_{k-2}^{|C_N|}\qquad q_\ell := \exp\left(-bT_{min} \exp\left[-2\ell DT_{max}\right]\right) \]
%
\noindent In particular, we have asymptotic consistency when $|C_N| \to \infty$:
%
\[\lim_{|C_N| \to \infty} \P(\cB \subseteq \cX_1) = 1,\]
%
\label{thm:canby_bipartition_cover_bounds_no_borrowing}
\end{theorem}
%
In particular, this implies if we run the ASTRAL as proposed in \red{identifiability canby paper},
restricted to the bipartition set $\cX_1$, this is a statistically consistent estimate 
of the true underlying phylogenetic tree $T$, and that we require $\exp(\Theta(k))$ characters to
achieve a bipartition cover with high probability. At stationarity, we typically have around $\lambda \mu^{-1}$ states in each leaf,
so roughly we can see $|\cX_1| \lesssim k \lambda \mu^{-1}$ grows only linearly in the number of leaves.

However, it is clear that $\cX_1$ is bad at generating large bipartitions.
 Namely, if $\phi_e = (A|B)$ the probability a 
  character born in edge $e$ has no deaths is exponentially
   small in $|A|$. To rectify this, we propose a few heuristics for expanding the size
    of $\cX_1$ so it has better coverage probabilities. A natural solution
     is to allow multiple states $\alpha$ to contribute:
%
\[\cX_m := \left\{\bigcup_{i=1}^r L_{\alpha_i}^{(c)}  \;: \; c \in C, \alpha_i \in \Sigma^{(c)}, r \leq m\right\}\]
%
\noindent It is easy to see that $\cX_n \subseteq \cX_m$ for $n \leq m$, so in
 particular $\cX_1 \subseteq \cX_m$. Hence asymptotic consistency using $\cX_m$ follows 
 from that of $\cX_1$. Moreover, $|X_m| = O(|C|^{m+1})$ so again we have some 
 control on the size relative to the number of characters. To avoid combining leaf sets from 
 completely different regions in the tree, we can also consider some sort of compatibility condition like
 $L_{\alpha_i}^{(c)} \cap L_{\alpha_j}^{(c)} \neq \emptyset$.

Alternatively, we can look for sets $A$ so that $L_\alpha$ takes up ``most''
 of $A$. Formally, defining some threshold $\tau$ we let
%
\[\cX_\tau := \left\{(A|S-A) \; : \; \exists \alpha \; \left(\frac{|A \setminus L_\alpha|}{|A|} < \tau\right)\right\}\]
%

\subsection{Formalizing Our Heuristic}

\noindent In this section, we propose various settings in which we can explicitly analyze the performance of our heuristic $X_\tau$


\subsubsection{Using Yule Trees}
\noindent Suppose a given state $\alpha \in \Sigma_N$ is born along an edge $e$. As we progress down the tree, every time we reach a branching point the number times $\alpha$ occurs in the current generation increases by one. When $\alpha$ dies in some branch, it decreases by one. 

Of course, this birth mechanism is completely deterministic and depends on the topology of the unknown species tree. But if we assume the underlying tree is itself generated via some random birth-process, we can explicitly analyze the expected fraction of leaves $\alpha$ reaches.

In particular, if we assume the underlying tree is a Yule tree with $\mathrm{Exp}(\gamma)$ branch lengths, then the number $A_t$ of $\alpha$ alive at a given point in time $t$ below the edge $e$ follows a birth-death process with birth and death rates $\gamma, \mu$ per individual respectively. It is easy to see:
%
\[\E A_t = e^{(\gamma - \mu)t}\]
%
\noindent If we let $N_t$ be the number of lineages in our yule tree, it is also easy to see:
%
\[E N_t = e^{\gamma t}\]
%
\noindent so it takes roughly $t_k := \gamma^{-1} \log(k)$ time to reach $k$ lineages. At this time, $\alpha$ occupies a fraction:
%
\[\frac{\E M_{t_k}}{\E N_{t_k}} = e^{-\mu t_k} = k^{-\mu/\gamma}\]
%
\noindent of the leaves approximately. This suggests choosing $\tau(k) \asymp 1- k^{-\mu/\gamma}$ if we hope to recover the given bipartition with decent probability.

\subsection{Properties of Immigration-Death Processes}

Let $\mathrm{ID}(\theta, \mu, \lambda)$ denote an immigration-death process $Y_t$ with $|Y_0| \sim \mathrm{Poisson}(\theta)$, birth rate $\lambda$, and death rate $\mu$ per individual. We start by understanding the population size in such a process. 

\begin{lemma}[Population Sizes in ID Processes]
    If $(Y_t)_{t \geq 0} \sim \mathrm{ID}(\theta, \mu, \lambda)$ then: 
    %
    \[|Y_t| \sim \mathrm{Poisson}(m(t)), \quad m(t) = \theta e^{-\mu t} + \frac{\lambda}{\mu}(1-e^{-\mu t}).\]
    %
    \noindent In particular, its stationary distribution is $\mathrm{Poisson}(\lambda/\mu)$. Moreover for $s < t$:
    %
    \[\E|Y_t \cap Y_s| = m(s) \cdot  e^{-\mu (t-s)}\]
    %
    \label{lem:pop_sizes_in_ID_processes}
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
    Kolmogorov's forward equations tell us:
    %
    \[\frac{d}{dt}p_k = \lambda p_{k-1} + \mu (k+1) p_{k+1} - (k\mu + \lambda) p_{k}, \quad p_k(0) = \frac{e^{-\alpha} \alpha^k}{k!}\]
    %
\noindent It is easy to see the Poisson probabilities $p_k(t) =  \frac{e^{-m(t)}m(t)^k}{k!}$ satisfy these equations, and hence $X(t) \sim \mathrm{Poisson}(m(t))$. The covariance formula follows from the fact:
\[Y_t = \mathrm{Binomial}(X_s, e^{-\mu (t-s)}) + Z, \quad |Z| \sim \mathrm{Poisson}(m_0(t-s))\]
\noindent where these two terms are independent. The formula follows from the law of total covariance. 
\end{proof}

\noindent It will be important to know how much time the Canby model spends with just a single state. For this purpose, it will be important to know how long it takes to go down from $n$ states to one. Since the dynamics of the ID process and Canby model essentially agree when there are more than one state, this reduces to studying the hitting times of our ID process. 

\begin{lemma}[Hitting Times in ID Process] Let:
%
\[\tau_n := \inf\{t > 0 : |Y_t| = 1\}\]
%
\noindent conditional on $|Y_0| = n$. Then: 
    %
    \[\E \tau_2 = \frac{\mu}{\lambda^2}(e^{\lambda / \mu} - 1 - \lambda/\mu)\]
    %
\noindent Moreover, its Laplace transform satisfies:
%
\[\frac{2\mu}{\lambda + 2\mu + s}\leq \E e^{-s\tau_2} \leq \frac{\lambda + 2\mu}{\lambda + 2\mu + s}\]
%
\end{lemma}

\red{cite the book Birth and Death BDP process with Applications.}

\begin{proof}
    Let $w_n := \E \tau_n$. Note by conditioning on the time of the first event we see:
    %
    \[w_n = \frac{1}{\lambda + n\mu} \left[1 + \lambda w_{n+1} + n\mu w_{n-1}\right], \quad w_1 = 0\]
    %
    \noindent 

    Next control the Laplace transform $\phi_n(s) := \E e^{-s \tau_n}$. We will rely on the classical relation between the Laplace transform of birth-death processes and continued fractions \red{cite something}. Conditioning on the first event time (either a birth or a death) we see:
    %
    \[\phi_n = \frac{\lambda}{\lambda + n \mu} \cdot \phi_{n+1} + \frac{n\mu}{\lambda + n \mu} \cdot \phi_{n-1}, \qquad \phi_1 \equiv 1\]
    %
    \noindent Noting that $\phi_3 \in [0,1]$ gives the desired bound on $\phi_2$. More generally, this gives an upwards recurrence for the ratios $r_n := \frac{\phi_n}{\phi_{n-1}}$ where $r_2 := \phi_2$:
    %
    \[r_n = \frac{n\mu}{\lambda + n\mu + s - \lambda r_{n+1}} \]
    %
    \noindent Iterating gives the continued fraction expansion:
    %
    \[r_{n} = \frac{n\mu}{\lambda + n\mu + s - \frac{\lambda(n+1)\mu}{\lambda + (n+1)\mu + s - ...}} \]
    %
    \noindent We can truncate this at even or odd convergents to get lower and upper bounds respectively on $r_n$. From these, we can bound $\phi_n(s) = \prod_{k=2}^n r_k$. 
\end{proof}

\noindent Note that the above implies in the low-polymorphism regime $\lambda \ll \mu$, we get $\E \tau_2 = \frac{1}{2\mu} + O(\lambda/\mu^2)$.

    \[|U \cap V| \leq_{st} \mathrm{Poisson}\left(m_{uv}\right), \quad  m_{uv} := \frac{\lambda}{\mu} \cdot \eta_{uv},\]
    %
    \noindent and analogously for $|X \cap Y|$. Chernoff's implies:
    %
    \[\P(\mathrm{Poisson}(m) \geq mt) \leq \exp(-m h(t)), \]
    %
    \noindent By union bound, for any $\gamma > 0$:
    %
    \[\P(|M_{uv||xy}| > t) \leq \P(|U \cap V| \geq \gamma\sqrt{t}) + \P(|X \cap Y| \geq \gamma^{-1}\sqrt{t}).\]
    %
    \noindent Setting $\gamma = \sqrt{m_{uv} /m_{xy}}$ weights the two terms according to their means, giving:
    %
    \[\leq \exp\left(-m_{uv} h \left(\sqrt{\frac{t}{m_{uv} m_{xy}}}\right)\right) + \exp\left(-m_{xy} h \left(\sqrt{\frac{t}{m_{uv} m_{xy}}}\right)\right)\]
    %
    \noindent from which the desired bound directly follows. 
\end{proof}

\end{proof}

\begin{proof}[Proof of Theorem \ref{thm:canby_bipartition_cover_bounds_no_borrowing}]

Fix an edge $e \in E(\cT)$ with $k_e$ descendants and a given character $c$. For ease of notation, we drop all the $(c)$ superscripts for now. If $\phi_e$ is the corresponding bipartition, we will lower bound the probability that $\phi_e = (L_\alpha|S-L_\alpha)$ for some $\alpha \in \Sigma$.

First, note that as the birth-rate is constant the number $N_e$ of non-homoplastic states born along the edge $e$ satisfies:
%
\[N_e \sim \mathrm{Poisson}(\lambda T_e \cdot \nu(\Sigma_N)) \succeq_{st} Poisson(\eta) \qquad \eta := \lambda T_{min} \nu(\Sigma_N)\]
%
\noindent where $T_e$ is the length of the branch $e$ and $T_{min}$ the minimum branch length in the tree. 

For a given non-homoplastic $\alpha$ born along the edge $e$, we can lower bound the probability it has no deaths below $e$. By Lemma \ref{lem:death_rate_prob_bounds} the probability $\alpha$ survives along a branch of length $T$ is at least $\exp(-\mu T)$. There are $2k_e - 1 \leq 2k_e$ edges (including $e$) that $\alpha$ can die on. Each is at most length $T_{max}$, so we can lower bound the probability $\alpha$ has no deaths at all by:
%
\[\P(\alpha \text{ has no deaths} \; |\alpha \in \Sigma_N, \alpha \text{ born on e}) \geq \exp(- \tau_{k_e}) \qquad \tau_{k_e} := 2\mu k_e T_{max}\]
%
\noindent Using this result followed by $N_e \succeq_{st} \mathrm{Poisson}(\eta)$ we see:
%
\[\P(\exists\alpha \; \phi_e = (L_\alpha, S-L_\alpha)) \geq \E_{n \sim N_e} \left(1-(1-\exp(-\tau))^n\right) \geq 1-\exp(-\eta e^{-\tau})\]
%
 From here, we can essentially piggy-back off my bipartition cover paper for the multispecies coalescent model. Namely, the caterpillar bound applied to our $|C|$ characters implies:
%
\[\P(\cB \subseteq \cX_1) \geq 1 - \sum_{\ell=2}^{k-2} \prod_{c \in C}\exp\left(-\eta^{(c)} e^{-\tau_{\ell}^{(c)}}\right) =  1 - \sum_{\ell=2}^{k-2} \exp\left(-\sum_{c \in C}\eta^{(c)} e^{-\tau_{\ell}^{(c)}}\right)\]
%
\noindent Note none of the above uses our boundedness assumptions on the birth or death rates $\lambda^{(c)}, \mu^{(c)}$. Using these assumptions on the birth and death rates, we see:
%
\[\P(\cB \subseteq \cX_1) \geq 1 - \sum_{\ell=2}^{k-2}q_\ell^{|C_N|} \geq 1-(k-3)q_{k-2}^{|C_N|} \qquad q_\ell := \exp\left(-bT_{min} \exp\left[-2\ell DT_{max}\right]\right)\]
%
\noindent Hence we get the desired exponential convergence of the converge probability.
\end{proof}
